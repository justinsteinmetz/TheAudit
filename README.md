# The Audit

A 20-minute classroom instrument disguised as a bucket list talk.

Students arrive having written personal bucket lists and expecting to discuss them. For five minutes, that's what happens. Then the deck renames itself, and the lists stop being work to be shared and become evidence to be examined: which of these desires did you originate, and which did you absorb?

## The arc

1. **Title** — the deliberately unfinished sentence
2. **Etymology** — *kick the bucket*; the genre is a response to mortality
3. **Why lists work** — one earnest beat, placed so it can be knocked over
4. **The question** — *But whose list did you write?*
5. **The reveal** — *This was never about bucket lists.* The deck renames itself: THE AUDIT
6. **Test 1 — The Postcard Test** (3:00) — tag every item that could appear on a travel poster or someone else's feed
7. **Test 2 — The Tomorrow Test** (3:00) — circle anything startable this week; impossible or just postponed?
8. **Test 3 — The Swap** (4:00) — exchange lists; defend the item that surprises your partner
9. **Harvest** — if you crossed out everything borrowed, what's left?
10. **Exit** — *How much of your future is currently being written by other people?*

No item is ever disqualified. The Postcard Test demands a defence, not a deletion — an item that survives the defence is more its owner's than it was before.

## Design

The house register: void background, Cormorant Garamond display, Inter body, grain overlay, single ember accent. The pacing is part of the instrument — one idea per slide, advanced by the teacher, with silence built into the sequence (slide 4 is intended to sit unspoken for several seconds).

## Pedagogy

A methodology exercise wearing a wellbeing topic. The transferable move — interrogate where a preference came from before treating it as yours — applies identically to ambitions, opinions, and media habits. The natural follow-up is running the Postcard Test against something other than bucket lists: career plans, or what students think they think about a current news story.

# The Audit lesson plan


**Context.** Students arrive having already written personal bucket lists. They expect a talk about bucket lists. The segment delivers that for five minutes, then turns: the real subject is where desires come from, and the lists become evidence to be examined rather than work to be praised.

**Objective.** Students leave able to distinguish between a desire they originated and a desire they absorbed — and having applied that distinction to their own written list.

**Materials.** Deck (`the-audit.html`, 10 slides, timers built in on slides 6–8). Students need their lists and a pen. Nothing else.

**Deck navigation.** Tap right side / → to advance, tap left quarter / ← to go back. Timers start and reset on click; they end on *discuss*.

## Timing

| Time | Slide | Beat | You |
|------|-------|------|-----|
| 0:00–1:00 | 1 | Entry | Title up as you're introduced. The sentence on screen is deliberately unfinished — "Everything you want to do before you—". Don't comment on it yet. |
| 1:00–3:00 | 2 | Etymology | The verbal beat the slide doesn't carry: "bucket list" comes from *kick the bucket* — to die. The term barely existed before the 2007 film. So the genre is a response to one problem: time runs out. Land it lightly; the class will enjoy being trusted with the morbid version. |
| 3:00–4:30 | 3 | Why lists work | One earnest beat, kept short: people who write goals down are measurably more likely to act on them. A list converts vague wanting into something nameable. Don't dwell — this slide is the setup being placed so it can be knocked over. |
| 4:30–5:00 | 4 | The question | Advance and stop talking. Let "But whose list did *you* write?" sit in silence for a few seconds before you continue. |
| 5:00–6:00 | 5 | The reveal | "This was never about bucket lists." Name the borrowed canon — skydiving, Northern Lights, Santorini — and frame the next ten minutes: we're going to audit the lists you've already written. |
| 6:00–9:00 | 6 | Test 1 — Postcard | Instructions on slide. Start timer (3:00). Students mark every item that could appear on a travel poster or someone else's feed. Stress *no judgement* — tagging, not deleting. Circulate. |
| 9:00–12:00 | 7 | Test 2 — Tomorrow | Timer (3:00). Circle anything startable this week in some small form. The diagnostic question to drop while circulating: "Is this item impossible, or just postponed?" |
| 12:00–16:00 | 8 | Test 3 — Swap | Timer (4:00). Pairs exchange lists. Each partner picks the item that most surprises them and asks "why this?" Listen for the difference between students who can answer and students who discover they can't. |
| 16:00–19:00 | 9 | Harvest | Discussion, hands: "Who found at least one item they suspect they borrowed?" Most hands go up — and it lands as funny rather than shaming because everyone's implicated. Take 3–4 responses on the screen question: if you crossed out everything borrowed, what's left? |
| 19:00–20:00 | 10 | Exit | Advance to the final slide, read it once aloud — "How much of your future is currently being written by other people?" — and stop. Do not resolve it. Hand back to the host teacher. |

## Facilitation notes

**The reveal is the hinge.** Everything before slide 5 should feel like a normal, pleasant guest talk. The flatter the first four minutes play, the harder the turn lands. Resist foreshadowing.

**Defending the postcard.** Some students will argue that skydiving genuinely is theirs. Let them — and make it explicit that winning that argument is the point. The Postcard Test doesn't disqualify items; it demands a defence. An item that survives the defence is more theirs than it was before.

**The Swap is where the learning happens.** The first two tests are private; the Swap is the only social pressure in the segment, and it's where students discover the difference between an item they chose and an item they copied — because one can be explained and the other can't. Give it the full four minutes even if you have to compress the harvest.

**Tone discipline.** The segment flirts with mortality and social conformity. Keep the register dry and curious, never solemn. The moment it sounds like an assembly, Year 9 closes.

---

## Contingencies

**Lists not in the room.** Two minutes at the top: reconstruct from memory, minimum five items. Frame it as part of the experiment — what you remember of your own list is already a filter.

**A very short or joke list.** Run the tests anyway. A three-item joke list audited seriously produces better discussion than a dutiful ten-item one — ask the Swap partner whether the jokes are hiding anything.

**Fast finishers on Tests 1–2.** Extension question, quietly: "Pick one unmarked item. Where did it come from? Trace it to a source — a person, a film, a feed."

**Discussion stalls at harvest.** Fall back to a count: hands for "more than half my list survived the Postcard Test," then hands for "less than half." The split itself becomes the discussion.

**Running over.** Cut harvest to one response and protect the exit slide. The segment can survive a thin discussion; it cannot survive ending mid-activity without the final question.
